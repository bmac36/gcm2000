Receiving a Job Offer

Congratulations! Your hard work and thoughtful job search strategy has paid off and you have just received a call or email that you are the preferred candidate! All ready to head over and start your first day? Not quite yet. The process of selection is not quite complete. A number of intermediary and administrative steps must be taken before you become an official employee of your new company.

Step

Conditional Offer

You find out that your official offer is dependent on certain conditions. The employer may send the conditional offer prior to sending details of the official job offer.

What's Involved

Examples of conditions include:

  • Reference Checks
    For more information on how to select, manage, and contact your references, see the Requesting a Reference section of the Alberta Learning Information Service website.
  • Verification of Qualifications
    What the employer may ask for often depends on your industry. The ways they verify your qualifications may depend on the type of employer.

Step

Official Offer

To signal an official offer, many employers will send you details of a job offer, items such as salary and benefits, hours of work, work location, schedule, and other details depending on your industry.

What's Involved

  • Employers often send an official offer package containing a contract with a signature and date line for you to sign and return to them.
    For an example of what an offer looks like, see the Job Offer
    Letter     section of the About Careers website.
  • Many employers will place a specific time limit on the offer. This means you need to respond within a set period of time or the offer will become void.

Step

Negotiating an Offer

Negotiating an offer with the employer before accepting.

What's Involved

  • Before signing and returning documentation, it is often customary to negotiate certain aspects of the job offer. Salary, vacation, and benefits are common negotiating points.
  • Successfully negotiating offers depends on a number of complex factors. To get a better sense of the complexity and factors, read the Harvard Business Review article 15 Rules for Negotiating a Job Offer .

Step

Notifying Your Current Employer

Notifying your current employer that you are leaving your current position to accept a new one.

What's Involved

  • If accepting the offer means you are leaving your current employer, you need to provide notice to your current employer. Best practice is to do this in writing.
  • A notice period is not required by law, but a minimum of two to four weeks of notice is considered customary. Less than two weeks could diminish your relationship with your current employer.
  • For more information on your rights and obligations when leaving your employer, read Quitting and Giving Notice: What Employees Need to Know .

Step
