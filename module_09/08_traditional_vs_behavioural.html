Traditional vs. Behavioural Interview Questions

Most interviews contain some version of a structured meeting in person or via telephone, with a series of questions and answers. Interview questions tend to fall into two broad categories: traditional (or typical) and behavioural. The purpose of these questions is to gather more information about the candidate, determine capabilities and suitability for the role, and assess how the candidate would fit with the corporate culture.

Traditional interview questions such as "Why are you interested in working for [insert company name here]?" can be much easier to answer than behavioural-based questions such as "Tell me how you handled a difficult situation." Traditional Interview Questions

  • Tend to be straightforward to answer (E.g. "When are you available for work?").
  • Focus on your personality, preferred ways of interacting with others, and how you would handle hypothetical situations.
  • Provides an opportunity for the interviewer and you to get to know each other and develop rapport.
  • Less stressful than behavioural-based interviewing.

Review the Typical Interview Questions & Responses document for examples of typical interview questions and guidelines for how to respond to them.

Behavioural-Based Questions

  • Tend to be more challenging to answer (E.g. "Describe a time when you took the initiative to solve a problem").
  • Focus on descriptions of real-world examples of your behavior, your work and your interpersonal manner, and how it could possibly relate to the work environment.
  • This is NOT hypothetical questioning (i.e. "what would you do if..."), you must provide details of a past situation.
  • Gives the interviewer the opportunity to predict your possible future behaviour based on your past behaviour.
  • More stressful than traditional (competency-based) interviewing.
