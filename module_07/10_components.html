Components of a Résumé

Before you customize your existing résumé for the job posting you have been given for the week, let’s review the various components that are present in a typical résumé. Does your résumé have these elements? Take some time to update your résumé after reviewing each to ensure that you have all the information you need before writing to the job posting.

Click on each component to learn more. There are a variety of ways to organize these components, depending on where you are in your career . We will examine the reverse chronological résumé and the functional résumé in this course.

Objective Statement

It is not always necessary to include an objective or career goal statement on your résumé, but if you do include one, make sure it is concise and meaningful. Compare these examples:

Weak:

To get a co-op work term.

Good:

To obtain a co-op work term from May to August 20xx.

Strong:

To apply my current education as an electronic technician student in a co-op work term from May to August 20xx.

Strongest:

To apply my strong research and analytical skills in the Junior Marketing role with Christie Digital during the May to August 20xx co-op work term.

The strongest statement applies to the specific job posting (job title and company), is time-specific, and incorporates transferable skills that would be a good fit for the job.

Highlights of Qualifications

This section assists the employer in determining your suitability for the position. Use it to describe your strongest technical and transferable skills related to the position to which you are applying. Employers will scan this section to determine which applicants to call for an interview. Include the following:

  • Proof of skills and experience related to the position description
  • Industry-related qualifications, designations, certifications
  • Related academic projects and accomplishments
  • Use and familiarity of specific software, tools, equipment, machinery and processes
  • Fluency in other languages

Education and Training

This section shows an employer that you have the training and education they need in a candidate. Be sure to include the following information:

  • Full program name (no abbreviations)
  • Institution name and dates attended
  • Relevant courses/projects that would interest an employer
  • Grade Point Average (GPA) if higher than 3.0
  • Academic awards (going back three to five years is sufficient)

For example:

Human Resources Management (post graduate)

September 20XX to Present

Conestoga College, Kitchener, ON

  • Program highlights include: HRIS software, Payroll, Financial and Managerial Accounting, and a Human Resources Capstone Project
  • Program meets the requirements for the CHRP designation

If you have complete any other training or professional development that would be applicable to the job posting, you would include that information here.

Employment History

For each position in your employment history, include your job title, the name of the company and the dates you worked there. Organize your employment in reverse chronological format, that is from Present to Past.

When you describe your previous employment, focus on your accomplishments, not your duties. Companies are in business to make a profit: what value do you bring as an employee? Emphasize how your presence in the previous organization brought change or made an impact.

When writing your work experience section, consider the following questions. See Writing Accomplishment Statements for more detail on how to write strong accomplishment statements.

  • What results did you attain?
  • Did you respond to customers effectively?
  • Did you work extra hours?
  • Did you increase productivity, morale or profits?
  • Were you given extra responsibilities?
  • Did you train or educate others?
  • Did you receive any awards, promotions, or letters of recognition?
  • Did you set any records, such as sales?
  • Did you improve old procedures or initiate new ones?
  • Were you asked to supervise others?
  • Did you work as a team member?
  • Were you asked to return to a job because of your good performance?
  • Did you increase productivity or profits?
  • Were you given additional responsibilities?

Related Experience

If you have experience that is related and relevant to the job posting but is not current, you can bring it to the employer’s attention by creating a separate section within your resume named Related Experience. If using this strategy, include only the related experience you’d like to highlight. Make sure that you use a consistent layout throughout, no matter what strategy you use.

Volunteer Experience

Volunteer work helps you to acquire transferable skills and develop a professional network while gaining experience, knowledge and confidence and contributing to the community.

Volunteer experience can be incorporated within your resume in the following ways:

  • In a related experience section
  • Under a separate volunteer section after employment history
  • Within the activities and interests section

Use accomplishment statements to describe what you learned in your volunteer experience.

Professional Memberships and Associations

Many disciplines, from Engineering to Theatre, have developed professional associations. These organizations/bodies serve to provide professional development, sometimes certification, and a central location for resources (job hunting, information, news) relevant to the discipline.

It is well worth becoming a student member (usually at a discounted rate) of the professional association(s) relevant to your field. You will have access to job leads, information, and news that you might not otherwise have access to, and it shows a potential employer that you are invested in your career.

Awards and Accomplishments

You can emphasize your academic, work or volunteer recognitions by creating a specific honours, awards, and accomplishments section of your résumé. Make sure to provide the following details:

  • Title of recognition or award and year awarded
  • Scope of the award: National, regional, or local

For example:

Co-op Student of the Year Award, Conestoga College

2013

  • Nominated by RBC Insurance in recognition of work term achievement, community involvement, and contribution to co-operative education

Interests and Activities

Your activities and interests and how you spend your free time can say a lot about what you value and how well you may fit into an organization. They show something about your attitudes as well, which are important to an employer when it comes to determining whether you are a match for their company and the particular job they are hiring for.

References

All job seekers should prepare and regularly update a list of professional references. Take some time to identify your references and be sure to have a list on hand throughout your job search. Contact those that you are planning to use as a reference. Listing references that you do not have their permission to do so is not appropriate or in your best interests as a job seeker. Two professional references and one personal are recommended.

In most cases, you will not include these within your résumé. You can, however, include a statement that you will provide references when requested. Your references should listed on a separate document that you either attach to the application (if requested) or that you bring to the job interview.
