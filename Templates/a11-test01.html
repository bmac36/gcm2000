Brown, well known for his work in the field of Sexuality and media links (2002, 2005), suggests that there are four ways that the media impacts us:

1.

1. By keeping sexual behaviour on public and personal agendas

News media keeps sexuality in our faces, making spectacles of issues in such a way as to make us think they are bigger and more common than they really are.

2.

2. By reinforcing a relatively consistent set of sexual and relationship norms

  • Consider the effects of soap operas. What roles are prescribed regularly for men and women? The defined social roles are stereotypical and adhere to societal ideals of what is feminine and masculine.
  • Sexual explicit and varied behaviours are exposed as normal and common, whether they are in fact or not.

3.

3. By rarely including sexually responsible models

When was the last time you saw a sitcom, movie or drama where passionate exchanges included discussion and practice of safe sex?

4.

4. By serving as a sexual super peer in the lives of adolescents

  • Early maturing adolescent girls are particularly affected by the sexualization of mass media. They have been found to seek out sexual content in movies, television, music and magazines. This finding is consistent across age and culture.
  • It is a concern because it is a powerful influence that lacks sexually healthy messages due to the nature of the sites teens most commonly visit.

The media has great power when it comes to the messages society will receive about human sexuality. Further research must assess specifically which circumstances, content and effects are within their control.
