Key Facts

  • Employment standards in Canada are enforced provincial Employment Standards Acts, legislation that sets the minimum standards that employers and employees must follow.
  • These Acts differ slightly in each province, so see the HR Policies & Employment Legislation site for information relevant to the province or territory in which you think you will be working.

Wages, Hours and Entitlements

Each province and territory sets their own minimum wage, and differences exist between the different regions. Visit the Minimum Wage Database to find the minimum wage in the different provinces and territories and see the Ministry of Labour website for more information.

With very limited exceptions, basic rules govern all employees in each province. For example, in Ontario, all employees are entitled to receive a minimum wage for time on the job, receive a vacation entitlement, work no more than 48 hours in one week, receive 8 hours of rest in between shifts, and receive standard meal breaks. Except for very limited circumstances (e.g., as part of schooling), unpaid working arrangements for workers are not legal.

For specifics about your entitlements (e.g., vacation, leaves, overtime, layoff or termination right), see the Employment Standards Act Guide .

Health and Safety in the Workpace

All workers have the right to work in a safe and healthy environment. A number of laws, programs, and services are in place to prevent unsafe work and mitigate risk, accidents, and job-related injury. To learn more about health and safety in the workplace, visit the Health and Safety website and review the Health and Safety at Work poster .

Remember:

  • Employers are required to take all reasonable precautions to protect the health and safety of workers; this often means providing equipment, training, and oversight.
  • Employers must also ensure that equipment, materials and protective equipment are maintained in good condition.
  • Employers are further required to provide information, instruction and supervision to protect worker health and safety.
  • You have the right to refuse unsafe work.

Workplace Equality

Employment equity involves achieving equality in all aspects of employment, including equal pay for different work of equal value (e.g., Pay Equity), and employment equality, which represents proactive employment practices for four designated groups: women, Aboriginal people, people with disabilities, and visible minorities. Workplace equality also includes accessibility standards for people with disabilities. Employers use impartial systems of recruitment, job evaluation, and accessibility standards to ensure employment conditions are equal among candidates and employees. Privacy

A number of laws Canada relate to privacy, and various government organizations and agencies are responsible monitor compliance related to privacy. Various laws apply depending on the nature and location of the organization. Government organizations are obliged to make certain information public, while protecting personal and sensitive information. See: M/FIPPA, PHIPPA and PIPEDA .

Discrimination

Canada has laws to protect workers from discrimination. In Canada you are also protected against discrimination based on age, sex, marital status, disability, or sexual orientation. To learn more, visit Canadian Human Rights Commission .
