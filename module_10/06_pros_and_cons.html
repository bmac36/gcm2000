Pros and Cons of Small and Large Employers

Business Structures

Click to reveal some of the benefits and drawbacks of working for small and large companies.

Small Employer

Large Employer

The organizational structure is flatter, giving you more opportunities to access and influence decision-makers.

The organizational structure likely features multiple layers of management, making it more difficult to influence key decision-makers.

You get to be involved in a variety of assignments at work because the work team is smaller; however, this may result in a heavier workload overall.

There is a potential that you will get pigeonholed in one defined role, where you focus solely on your area of expertise.

Your benefits and salary may not be as generous (due to cost constraints).

You are likely to receive a competitive salary and robust benefits package.

There’s a great deal of visibility – everyone can see what you do and you are expected to help others whenever needed.

It’s easier to get lost in the crowd and seem “faceless”, making it necessary to market yourself internally in order to get noticed.

If the owners or top managers make bad business decisions, your job could be at risk.

A single bad decision by management is unlikely to put your job at risk, making the employer much more stable over time.

Startups vs. Established Firms

Some small businesses fall under the category of “startup” – a company that has just been founded with plans to rapidly grow. Working for these types of companies can be radically different from working for an established firm, for many of the reasons mentioned above. Both have benefits and drawbacks and it’s important to educate yourself before deciding your preference.
