Values

Values are your personal guideposts. Ideally, these principles exert enormous influence over your choices regarding work. They are informed by your upbringing, role models, expectations and experiences. Following them generates a sense of fulfillment. If you are in a situation that requires you to act against your principles, you will become unhappy, stressed, and suffer burnout (Watters & O’Connor, 2001, p.65).

Whatever aligns with your values also drives satisfaction in the workplace. For example, you may value money or performing work that contributes to the good of society.

Interests

Job satisfaction and success is closely linked to being interested in what you do. With an eye on future fulfillment, consider accomplishments, tasks or projects at which you excelled and that created a sense of pride and satisfaction.

Ask yourself: what do I like to do? Likes, dislikes, and indifferences regarding various occupations and career-relevant activities are important determinants of career choice. Make note of what attracts your attention.

Action Item: Your Self-Assessment and Reflection Summary includes a short list of interests to consider. Check off the ones of most importance to you in section 6.

Personality

One of the most important factors in determining your happiness and success in a job is understanding your personal attributes, also referred to as personality. Everyone has a distinct set of attributes comprised of individual traits: thoughts, feelings, behaviours, and attitudes. Are you easy going? Strong willed? Practical? Be honest when you assess your thoughts, feelings, behaviours, and attitudes.

Many personality tests exist, such as Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI), which can tell you how you process information, solve problems, make decisions, and communicate, among other factors. These tests are based on different personality theories, which are pretty easy to understand at a basic level.

You may want to familiarize yourself with some of the more popular theories.

Skills

A skill is a demonstrated ability to do something well. Skills can be learned and developed in a variety of ways: through academic or vocational training, self-study, hobbies, or on-the-job activities. This differs from knowledge, which is the theoretical or practical understanding of a subject.

Examples of skills include public speaking, supervising, and analyzing data. The great thing about skills is that you can transfer these abilities to different industries or even different roles. For example, if you enjoy analyzing data, you can perform this at a financial organization or at a university.

Along with transferable skills (also known as “soft” skills) you may have specific technical (or “hard”) skills that are job-specific and learned through experience or training.
