Apply the Four Step Research Process

Sourcing and using Labour Market Information wisely can help you to determine local opportunities and assist with making good choices when embarking on a job search. It can also help you to solidify your career strategy.

Think back to last week, where you learned about the four steps to research success: Collect It, Organize It, Analyze It, and Use It.

Click on each step to learn how to apply this model when researching Labour Market Information.

Collect It

Collect information by focusing on a specific trend, field, or industry of interest. Look at a large number of resources. Consult with a number of different types of sources (like newspapers, reports, people), and be creative in tracking down the information.

Organize It

Organize your information by noting references and keeping a list or compiling a document of your findings. You must think carefully about where information is coming from. Official sources of information, like the government, a college or university, or a professional organization will have information that is probably more reliable than the opinions of one person. If you find the same information repeated in multiple sources, it’s more likely to be true.

Analyze It

To come up with a career strategy based on labour market information, start analyzing the information you have collected from the various sources on one cluster of careers, industry, or sector. Think actively about what the information is telling you. Form hypotheses about your sector and test these hypotheses to draw conclusions. Look for patterns in what each source is telling you. To get started, refer to this list of guiding questions.

Use It

Share your findings with your network of family, friends, co-workers, classmates, instructors, and contacts. Refer to your research notes before meeting potential employers, attending networking events, or an interview. Also use your research to evaluate employment opportunities.

Guiding Questions

  • What do the National occupation Classification clusters tell you about the outlook for your chosen occupation? What skills, qualifications, and abilities are required? What’s the overall outlook?
  • Are you are in a sector that is expanding, diversifying, or contracting?
  • Are jobs in the industry/sector focused around specific regions? Do you live in one of these regions? Do you intend to move, and if so, what’s the outlook in your new location?
  • What is the demographic profile? Are workers aging? What’s the gender balance in the workplace?
  • What’s the popular image of this industry? How are these jobs portrayed by the media? What stereotypes do you hold regarding these opportunities?
  • What information do you have about specific opportunities? Sample postings, titles, salaries? What are sample entry-level jobs and career paths? Have you had one of these jobs in the past?
  • Is your industry affected by cultural or social trends? What is trending right now that could affect demand or production? Is the trend likely to be temporary or long-standing?
  • What credentials are required? Do you possess these credentials? Do you have plans to upgrade your skills in the future?

Next, look at another cluster of careers, industries, or sectors to compare. Think laterally about opportunities, and compare the outlook, trends, and requirements your different fields you have studied. Take careful notes to record this information for future reference.
