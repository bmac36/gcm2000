Making Communication Accessible

Being able to communicate on a basic level is something many of us take for granted. Speaking and understanding, reading and writing are skills that most of us use every day. We communicate to express our feelings, thoughts and opinions, to ask questions, and to give information. We do this with lots of people and for many different reasons.

Accessibility Standards in Ontario

There are thousands of people in Ontario who have disabilities that affect hearing, speaking, reading, writing, and/or understanding, and who use different ways to communicate than people who do not have these disabilities. They have the same rights to communicate as people who don’t have any visible disabilities. These rights are protected under the Ontario Human Rights Code. In addition, accessibility standards under the Accessibility for Ontarians with Disabilities Act, 2005 (AODA) set out legal requirements to ensure that Ontario is accessible to people with disabilities.

Familiarizing myself with the AODA guidelines made me much more sensitive to the needs of the disabled. I now use high-contrast colour for backgrounds, refrain from using coloured text, and keep to standard fonts in sizes that are easy to read. Word, PowerPoint, and Excel have built-in accessibility checkers that also tell me if there are any issues that could make it difficult for a disabled person to read what I’ve written.

Jenn
