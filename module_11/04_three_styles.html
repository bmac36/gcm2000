Three Communication Styles

Different communication styles can help you to convey your message. You may need to adjust your style depending on your personality, organizational culture, workplace customs, message, and audience.

Click on the three styles below. As you read through the descriptions, ask yourself:

  • What is your preferred/natural communication style?
  • In which situations would you apply each communication style?
  • Which style should you strive to adopt in the workplace?

Passive

Passive Communication is a style of communication that includes indirectness, avoidance, and pleasing others at the expense of your own needs or beliefs. It is usually achieved by frowning, whispering, crying, or saying nothing.

Purpose:

To avoid conflict and withhold your true feelings to the extent that the person you are communicating with does not know your true feelings.

Pro:

If avoidance is truly necessary, this is an effective way of not dealing with conflict.

Con:

This style seldom results in you getting what you want.

Assertive

Assertive Communication is a style of communication where you speak up for your rights, and speak directly and honestly about what you want or need.

Purpose:

To be respectful of others and not be angry or frustrated, but remain clear about what you want yourself.

Audience:

Professional, friendly contacts, coworkers, friends and colleagues.

Pro:

Self-confidence increased, respect for others maintained but your desire is communicated clearly and more likely to achieve desired result with both parties saving face.

Con:

Can feel uncomfortable to speak up or to limit strong emotions. With assertive communication, you need to balance between aggressive and passive.

Aggressive

Aggressive Communication is a style of communication where you use control over others to dominate, push, blame, and humiliate the audience. It is usually achieved through shouting, clapping hands, waving arms, writing in all-caps, using offensive language, or otherwise acting in an intimidating manner.

Purpose:

Communicating in an extreme situation, requiring a crisis response (such as a disaster or personal attack).

Audience:

Someone attacking you or a passive group in an emergency situation.

Pro:

Enables you to win and dominate others.

Con:

Aggressive communication is "zero sum," which means that you communicating your control means someone else is surrendering their control. Controlling others can be seen as negative and mean. If you are aggressive, your message may be misunderstood.
