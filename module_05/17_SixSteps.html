Networking Successfully

5

Six Steps to Follow

Consider the following six steps regarding how to succeed with informational interviews:

How to

1) Collect your Research Regarding Career Fields and Trends

  • Gather the research you have collected on careers, industries, employers, and trends using Internet and print resources.
  • Prepare summaries of this research and memorize the information.

2) Identify People to Interview

  • Pursue your informal network first – prepare to set up your first interview with your network that you’ve already established.
  • Call organizations directly or visit their website for the name of someone working within a particular area of interest.
  • Read relevant newspaper and magazine articles to find out names of people in your industry.
  • Identify names of Conestoga alumni. Conestoga graduates will often take a special interest in Conestoga students. Utilize LinkedIn to connect with alums.

3) Prepare for the Interview

  • Prepare to make a positive impression.
  • Practice your introduction and also prepare to discuss your reasons for contacting this person. Use this as a way to introduce yourself and define the context of the meeting.
  • Plan open-ended questions. Consider the following:
    • What are your main responsibilities as a...?
    • What is a typical day (or week) like for you?
    • What do you like most about your work?
    • What do you like least about your work?
    • What kinds of problems do you deal with?
    • What kinds of decisions do you make?
    • How does your position fit within the organization/career field/industry?
    • How does your job affect your general lifestyle?
    • What current issues and trends in the field should I know about/be aware of?
    • What are some common career paths in this field?
    • What kinds of accomplishments tend to be valued and rewarded in this field?
    • What related fields do you think I should consider looking into?
    • How did you become interested in this field?
    • How did you begin your career?
    • How do most people get into this field? What are common entry-level jobs?
    • What steps would you recommend I take to prepare to enter this field?
    • How relevant to your work is your undergraduate major?
    • What kind of education, training, or background does your job require?
    • What skills, abilities, and personal attributes are essential to success in your job/this field?
    • What is the profile of the person most recently hired at my level?
    • What are the most effective strategies for seeking a position in this field?
    • Can you recommend trade journals, magazines or professional associations which would be helpful for my professional development?
    • If you could do it all over again, would you choose the same path for yourself? If not, what would you change?
    • I’ve read that the entry-level salary range for this field is usually in the range of ______? Does this fit with what you’ve seen? (Don’t ask about the person’s actual salary.)
    • What advice would you give someone who is considering this type of job (or field)?
    • Can you suggest anyone else I could contact for additional information?

4) Initiate Contact

  • Contact the person by phone or email (see examples below).
  • Mention how you found their name (e.g., at an event, via social media, referral).
  • Ask if they’re available and it’s a good time to talk for a few minutes.
  • Make it clear that that you are looking for advice, not searching for a job.
  • Ask for a convenient time to have a 10-15 minute appointment.
  • Be ready to ask questions on the spot if the person says it is a good time for them and that they won’t be readily available otherwise.

Example of telephone request for informational interview

Hello. My name is Robert Robertson and I'm a second year student in the Management Studies program at Conestoga College. Is this a good time for you to talk for a moment? I heard you speak at the employer forum sponsored by the Conestoga Career Centre last semester. Although I am not currently looking for a job, I have become very interested in Management Studies and would like to find out as much as I can about the field. Would it be possible to schedule 20 to 30 minutes with you at your convenience to ask you a few questions and get your advice on how best to prepare to enter the field?

Example of email request for an informational interview

Subject: Informational Interview

Dear Mr. Smith:

I am a second-year student in the Management Studies Program at Conestoga College, and spoke with you at the P4E Career Fair in February.

Before starting my career, I am very interested in learning all I can about typical career paths in the management field and what skills I might need to develop during my final year of study at Conestoga. I would greatly appreciate 15 to 20 minutes of your time to ask you a few questions about your position as a Management Specialist with ABC Corporation, as well as other opportunities in the field.

Thank you for your consideration. I will contact you next week to arrange a convenient time to meet with you.

Sincerely,
Robert Robertson

5) Conduct the Informational Interview

  • Before arriving, make sure you know the exact time and location. Write down the person’s name, title, and duties. Practice your introduction.
  • Dress neatly and appropriately, as you would for a job interview. Arrive on time or a couple of minutes early. Introduce yourself clearly and with a handshake, as you would at a networking event.
  • Write out some of the questions you intend to ask in a notebook and keep that handy for reference.
  • Again make it clear that your objective is to get information and advice, not a job.
  • Provide your introduction, with the goal of making a good impression.
  • Be prepared to take charge of the interview by asking the questions you prepared in advance, but let the meeting proceed naturally, and encourage the interviewee to do most of the talking. Don’t interrupt.
  • Listen well and show genuine interest in what they have to say, and take notes.
  • Respect their time. Keep the appointment length within the time that you requested and don’t take much longer or shorter.
  • Ask the person if you may contact them again in the future with other questions, and ask for names of other people to talk to for additional information or a different perspective (this can be another way to grow your network).
  • Thank them for their time and depart the meeting confidently.

6) Follow-up

  • Right after the interview, add to the notes you have been keeping for networking events. Write down what you learned, what new questions you have, and list your reactions in terms of how the industry, employer, and occupation fits with your VIPS.
  • If the person you interviewed provided additional contacts, record their contact information. Consider sending informational interview requests to them.
  • Send a follow-up message within 1-2 days to express your appreciation for the time and information given.

Example of a follow-up message:

Dear Mr. Smith:

I appreciate your meeting with me yesterday to talk about the work that you do as the Management Specialist. I now have a much better understanding of the field, particularly within a large organization like ABC Corporation.

It was helpful to hear that an entry-level position often requires some experience with budget management. I have decided to take on those responsibilities for the executive council of my student association as a treasurer. I have also called your colleague, Ms. Jones, at XZY Incorporated to make an appointment to talk with her.

It was very helpful to have had the opportunity to talk with you. Thank you again for your time and advice.

Sincerely,

Robert Robertson

Keep in touch with the person, especially if you had an effective interaction. If you connected over your program, perhaps let them know when you graduate. If you follow up with somebody else, let them know that you followed up on their advice and how things are going as a result. This relationship could become an important part of your network.
