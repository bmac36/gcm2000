Teamwork and Personalities

Individual personality types can have a big influence on how well a team is functioning. Depending on personality and preference, team members may have very different ideas around problem-solving, planning, distributing work, tracking progress, and communicating with one another. One member’s preferred method of communication, for example, may be a method that is ineffective for another team member. Consequently, many people have had negative experiences working on teams. Finding a way to decrease team conflict because of personality is key to building effective teams.

Follow these four steps to develop teams that harness the power of personality types:

Steps

1.

1. Commit to self-assessments and continually monitor yourself to see if your VIPS change over time.

2.

2. Review personality markers and understand how they differ from other types.

3.

3. Highlight some of the strengths and drawbacks of your personality profile for your team, to help them understand who you are and how you will fit with the team. Take the time to learn about other team members’ profiles, strengths, and drawbacks.

4.

4. Work with the team to develop roles that play to the strengths of personality differences, maintaining a high degree of team effectiveness.

Roles Within Teams

Consider these four team roles , how they fit with personality markers, and what they can do for a team:

1) Leaders — Who needs to be in charge?

  • Personality markers: Driven, extroverted, confident, focused
  • A leader role can help teams when they understand each team member’s differences and how they fit together, listens to others and understands the issues systemically, delegates work properly, and builds a shared vision towards completion.
  • A leader role can hurt teams when they get caught up in being in charge and become bossy or tyrannical. They may only listen to themselves or only value their own opinions, and creativity is stifled when expectations are unreasonable.

2) Creative Thinkers — Who's the daydreamer?

  • Personality markers: imaginative, creative, innovative, inventive
  • A creative thinker role can help teams explore alternatives or find new ways to do things. Creative types are usually good communicators and inspire others with their optimism, which is good for others in terms of finding ways to "think outside the box".
  • A creative thinker role can hurt teams when their impatience and impulsive thinking leads them to rush from idea to idea without committing to any one path, or considering the consequences before acting.

3) Analysts — Who can't stop asking questions?

  • Personality markers: detail-oriented, planning, research-focused, cautious
  • An analyst role can help teams plan their progress in detail by understanding each aspect of the task at hand, carefully researching the steps, and asking many questions about expectations. Typically cautious about adopting a new idea, they can prevent the team from making unnecessary mistakes.
  • An analyst role can hurt teams by holding up the team’s progress because of the many questions, excessive planning, and concern over taking risks. Being cautious about new approaches may lead to conflict with other roles too, especially if others really want to adopt a new idea.

4) Pleasers — Who wants everybody to get along?

  • Personality markers: group-oriented, accommodating, dependable
  • A pleaser role can help teams with conflict by helping everyone get along through their social approach to conflict resolution. Since pleasers support unity and cohesion, they can play a key role in helping to keep the team together.
  • A pleaser role can hurt teams because they may not be forthcoming about opinions or feelings, leading to reduced morale or inefficiencies. Minimizing conflict isn’t always optimal, especially since ideas may need to be strenuously debated before being adopted

Reflective Questions

  1. Which role is a fit for your personality?
  2. How will you help teams with your strengths and minimize the drawbacks of your personality type?
