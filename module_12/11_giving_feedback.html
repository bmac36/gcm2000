Giving Feedback

Effective feedback provides both instruction and motivation. It encourages better behaviour in the future. To accelerate performance growth in yourself and your our co-workers, itâ€™s important to learn how to properly give and receive feedback.

Five Steps for Giving Feedback

Management consultant Tim Burns outlines the following five steps for giving feedback .

In general, feedback to employees should be timely. If improvement needs to be made in their performance, the sooner they find out about it the sooner they can correct the problem. If employees have reached or exceeded a goal, the sooner they receive positive feedback, the more rewarding it is to them. Before giving feedback make sure you remind yourself why you are doing it. Focus on improvement; the purpose is to improve the situation or performance. You won't accomplish that by being harsh, critical, or offensive.

1. Ask for permission

How to start the dialogue

"Can I give you some feedback?"

"I would like to give you some feedback, is now okay?"

"Would you like some feedback?" (if a peer)

Why do this step?

  • Prepare the person to hear what you have to say.
  • Find out if the timing is good — if not, do it at another time.

2. Share your Observavtions

How to start the dialogue

"I've observed/noticed... "

"When you X..." (more informal)

Example: "I noticed you interrupted Matt in our meeting to make your point."

Why do this step?

  • Set the context.
  • Share observations related to behaviour or issue only.
  • Establish that this is not personal or judgemental.

3. Check/Pause for Understanding

How to start the dialogue

"Do you remember that?"

"Did I describe that correctly?"

Why do this step?

  • Receiver can acknowledge the observation and potentially arrive at the insight themselves.
  • Make sure that you are talking about the same thing.
  • Disagreement here could mean that your feedback may not be well-received.

4. Describe the Effect it has on you

How to start the dialogue

"The effect it has on me is..."

"It made me feel..."

"It makes me think the following will happen..."

Example: "I believe that it stifles the discussion and I feel we are not getting the full team's contribution."

Why do this step?

  • It shows how you were affected as a statement of fact.
  • Your perceptions and feelings matter because you make your decisions based on them.
  • Share understanding of broader impact.

5. Suggest (or as for) Alternate Behaviour

How to start the dialogue

"I think X would be more effective."

"Can you please..."

"What do you think you should do?"

Example: "In your next meeting, wait until Matt is finished or he asks for your input before voicing your opinion."

Why do this step?

  • Only a different behaviour will achieve a different outcome.
  • A specific and tangible alternative option increases the likelihood of behaviour change.
  • Grow to understand the other person's point of view more clearly.
