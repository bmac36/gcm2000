Intergenerational Groups

Age is an important characteristic of our identity.


People from the Same Generation

Two people who are close in age will typically share the same cultural experiences at roughly the same time in their lives. People that are born within a certain timeframe are considered to share a "cohort" and be part of the same "generation". Even across geographic, social, or gender boundaries, two people of the same generation may share similar values or expectations.

People from Different Generations

Alternatively, two people that are far apart in age means they may not have the same shared cultural experiences, and could have very different values or expectations. These two would be considered from different generations or “intergenerational.” In Canada, recent debates over intergenerational relationships, biases, and discrimination in the workplace represents a key issue in diversity and inclusion.

Four Generational Cohorts in Canada

Today’s workforce contains more generations than any other time in history. Most people in Canada today fit into one of the four generational cohorts: Traditionalists, Baby Boomers, Generation X, and Millennials.

Discrimination Based on Age

Unfortunately, discrimination based on age (sometimes called "Ageism") is a common occurrence in Canada. Ageism stems from the biases and prejudices that some in a generational cohort have regarding members of another cohort.

For example, many hold the view that Baby Boomers are bad with technology, and inflexible with change management. Many see Generation X as collectively shallow, sensitive and consumerist. Moreover, a common prejudice is that Millennials are lazy, "entitled", and not willing to work hard for successes. These prejudices are generally based on false stereotypes that don’t match reality and lead to cases of discrimination in the workplace.

Building Strong Intergenerational Teams

Despite negative views that generational cohorts may have regarding each other, having teams made up of members from different cohorts actually leads to stronger teams . In spite of prejudices that make us think the opposite, diversity makes teams more effective and perform better than teams made up of members from one generational cohort. Dispelling stereotypes and biases can lead to a more effective and enjoyable work experience for all. Also having a basic understanding of the expectations and motivators for each generation can help you to better understand, relate to, and work effectively with all generations.
