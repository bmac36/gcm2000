Thriving in a Knowledge Economy

What do you think it will take for companies to remain competitive in today’s knowledge economy ?

If you guessed "a highly-skilled and knowledgeable workforce", you are right!

Anwar

Consider the following finding taken from the Ontario Chamber of Commerce (OCC) report Protecting Our Most Valuable Resource: The Business Case for Lifelong Learning and Job-Based Training, based a 2010 survey of Ontario employers on the subject of workforce learning:

"The rapid turnover of technology and ever expanding network of data and information which underpin the knowledge economy have led to a reevaluation of the importance of knowledge to the economic process. Economists now conclude that human capital—the ideas, skills, and expertise of people—is a fundamental driver of economic growth (Ontario Chamber of Commerce, 2011, p. 4)."

21st Century Skills

The specific transferable skills that employers are seeking today are generally termed "21st century skills".

The Partnership for 21st Century Learning defines 21st century skills under the three main categories:

Learning and Innovation Skills

  • Critical Thinking
  • Creative Thinking
  • Collaboration
  • Communication

Information, Media and Technology Skills

  • Information Literacy
  • Media Literacy
  • Information and Communication Technology Literacy (ICT)

Life and Career Skills

  • Flexibility
  • Initiative
  • Social and Cross-Cultural Skills
  • Productivity and Accountability
  • Leadership and Responsibility

You can see how the knowledge economy, defined by a fast rate of technological change and ever-increasing amounts of information, demands a new kind of workforce with a specialty skill set. This requires a response from you! To remain effective, you must continue to learn and grow. In the next topic we’ll explore how committing to lifelong learning can help you to keep pace with and even stay ahead of the competition.
