Steps to Becoming a Lifelong Learner

What will it take for you to become a lifelong learner as it relates to your professional development? One approach is to continuously apply the three steps outlined below.

Expand each panel to learn more.

1. Inventory Your Knowledge and Skill Set

Becoming a lifelong learner requires you to keep track of your skill development as you progress in your career. As skill improvement is a key training objective for organizations looking to boost productivity, being aware of your skills as you progress through your career is important.

You need to be able to inventory your current skills and knowledge before you can assess where further skills and knowledge are required to promote a successful career path. Understanding yourself started when you assessed your VIPS in the second week of this course. Continue to revisit and revise your VIPS as you progress through your career.

2. Identify Needed Skills and Knowledge

The second step is to identify the specific skills and knowledge you need to keep pace and stay ahead of the competition in your chosen occupation and field.

Here are some of the approaches to identifying needed skills and knowledge that have already been discussed in this course:

  • Conduct trend and labour market research, and occupational research.
  • Attend networking events and other professional meetings.
  • Meet with leaders and other people of influence (e.g., through informational interviews).
  • Examine the essential skills and work habits identified by government for employability, especially those related to your chosen occupation:
    • Essential Skills (Federal)
    • Essential Skills (Ontario)
    • Work Habits (Ontario)
    • Ontario Skills Passport (Use to appraise and track your skill development.)

3. Determine Your Learning Approach

Lastly, consider what approach you will take to learning. Some of the approaches to continuous learning outlined on the federal governmentâ€™s essential skills site are:

  • Learn on the job.
  • Learn through formal training.
  • Learn through self-study.

Knowing how you learn best (e.g., classroom or on your own) and the best sources of information (e.g., a college, fellow employee, or print/online resource) can also help you to make informed decisions about the best learning approach to take.
