Assessing Intrapersonal Skills

 

How can you assess your intrapersonal skills? Review the three recommended approaches below.


Expand each panel to learn more.

Keep a Journal

Keeping a journal means writing down your daily thoughts, activities, encounters, and notable events as you pursue your goals. Keep these records in a book or notepad, or on your computer or tablet. The more you write down your thoughts and behaviours related to your goals, the more you will be able to identify your behaviour patterns. For example, do you always feel defeated if somebody says no to you, and do you treat this like a situational barrier? Are you easily flattered, which creates impulses that hurt your pursuit of your goals? Identify the moments that you feel very strong emotions, like love, hate, frustration, or joy. After identifying your patterns of emotion, begin to ask yourself what stimulates these patterns. When do you start feeling these ways? Can you connect past experiences to your current emotional state?

Exercise Mindfulness Meditation

Being mindful means freeing your mind from distractions and focusing on the present. Mindfulness is often associated with meditation or other exercises designed to mitigate negative emotions and build our self-regulatory skills. Meditation is also a scientifically-proven way to increase self-awareness, as it has been linked to increased brain activities in the areas that associated with thinking, awareness, decision-making, and compassion.

Consciously Self-Reflect

With deliberate action, turn your mind to "metacognition" – think about how you are thinking. Plan, monitor, and assess the ways that your thinking guides your behaviours. In tandem with keeping a journal and practicing mindfulness, explicit self-reflection will train your body and mind to have better emotional awareness and regulation.
